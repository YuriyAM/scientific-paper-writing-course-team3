Topic

Selection and delimitation of topic

A strong scientific paper begins with a topic that is relevant, researchable, and sufficiently delimited. A broad or vague topic usually leads to weak research questions and an unfocused methodology.

Preliminary research before selection

Criterion Guiding question Decision
Literature density Is the topic over-studied or underrepresented? Identify a narrower niche, comparison, or new application.
Complexity Is the topic too broad for one paper? Limit by region, period, method, dataset, case, variable, or theory.
Data access Can reliable evidence be obtained? Avoid questions that cannot be answered with available data.
Contribution Can the paper clarify, compare, test, evaluate, or explain something? Define the expected contribution before writing.

From broad to focused topic

Too broad More focused Improvement
Circular economy in agriculture Valorisation of winery by-products as organic amendments in vineyard soil management Specifies sector, material, application and context.
Climate change and wine Effects of drought adaptation practices on grape composition in a selected wine region Defines phenomenon, response strategy and measurable outcome.
Common risk A topic may sound impressive but still be unsuitable if it cannot be transformed into answerable research questions.
Justification

Problem, gap and contribution

A scientific paper needs a clear reason for existing. That reason must be visible at three levels: why the topic matters, what remains insufficiently understood, and what the paper adds.

Context

Shows why the topic matters.

Typical wording: “In recent years, increasing attention has been paid to...”

Problem / gap

Shows what remains unclear or insufficiently investigated.

Typical wording: “However, limited evidence is available regarding...”

Contribution

Shows what the paper adds.

Typical wording: “This paper contributes by evaluating / comparing / modelling...”

Gap statement formula Although X is important, Y remains insufficiently understood; therefore, this paper aims to Z by using A and producing B.

Types of research gaps

Gap type Description Contribution
Empirical gap Insufficient data for a region, case, sector, or material. Generate new evidence.
Methodological gap Existing methods are limited, outdated, or non-comparable. Apply, compare, or refine methods.
Practical gap Knowledge exists, but implementation guidance is weak. Develop recommendations or decision criteria.
Alignment

Research objectives and research questions

Objectives state what the paper intends to do. Research questions state what it intends to answer. They must be logically connected, methodologically feasible, and visible again in the results section.

Objective verbs

Objective verb Use when the paper intends to...
Evaluate Assess influence, suitability, or effectiveness.
Compare Identify similarities and differences between treatments, methods, or cases.
Determine Establish a value, relationship, condition, or effect.
Analyse Examine patterns, relationships, mechanisms, or structures.
Develop Produce a framework, model, index, protocol, or recommendation.

Question types

Question type Example wording Suitable evidence
Descriptive What are the main characteristics of...? Measurements, classifications, descriptive statistics.
Comparative How do A and B differ with respect to...? Comparative tables, statistical tests, structured comparison.
Explanatory To what extent does X influence Y? Experimental data, models, correlations, causal reasoning.

Alignment matrix

Objective Research question Method Expected result
Evaluate the effect of treatment variants. How do treatment variants influence selected indicators compared with the control? Field experiment, laboratory analysis, statistical comparison. Tables or figures showing differences among treatments.
Rule Each objective should correspond to at least one research question, one method, and one result.
Architecture

Structure of a scientific paper

The structure of a scientific paper is not only a formatting rule. It is a communication logic that guides the reader from research problem to evidence, interpretation, and contribution.

Section Main function Central question answered
Title Identifies topic, scope, and focus. What is this paper about?
Abstract Summarises aim, methods, results, and implications. Is this paper relevant?
Introduction Presents context, problem, gap, aim, and questions. Why was the study needed?
Literature Review Synthesises prior knowledge and identifies the gap. What is known and missing?
Methodology Explains design, data, methods, and validity. How was the study conducted?
Results Reports findings objectively. What was found?
Discussion Interprets findings and explains significance. What do the results mean?
Conclusion Summarises answers, contribution, and outlook. What is the final message?
References Documents sources and supports traceability. Where does the knowledge come from?
Structural principle The introduction should prepare the literature review; the literature review should justify the methodology; the methodology should generate the results; the discussion should interpret the results; and the conclusion should answer the research questions.
Opening package

Title, abstract and introduction

The opening parts of the paper should help readers understand the topic, relevance, research problem, and expected contribution quickly and accurately.

Title

Weak title Stronger title Reason
Sustainable Agriculture and Waste Organic Valorisation of Winery By-Products in Vineyard Soil Management Specifies material, sector, and application.

Abstract

Component Purpose
Motivation / problem Explain why the research was needed.
Aim State what the paper investigates.
Methods Summarise how the study was conducted.
Results Report the most important findings.
Implications Explain contribution or relevance.

Introduction sequence

  1. Context: Introduce the broad field and relevance.
  2. Problem: Explain the uncertainty or limitation that motivates the study.
  3. Gap: Indicate what previous studies have not sufficiently addressed.
  4. Aim and questions: Present objectives and research questions.
  5. Contribution: Clarify how the paper adds to knowledge, method, or practice.
