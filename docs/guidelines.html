Literature

Literature overview

A literature overview is not a list of summaries. It should synthesise prior knowledge, explain the state of the art, identify the gap, and position the paper within a scholarly conversation.

Literature workflow

Search
Screen
Classify
Compare
Synthesise
Identify Gap

Search and documentation

Element Recommended action
Databases Use field-relevant databases such as Google Scholar, Scopus, Web of Science, PubMed, or institutional repositories.
Keywords Combine core concepts, synonyms, methods, region names, and outcome variables.
Inclusion criteria Define publication years, language, relevance, article type, and methodological quality.
Record keeping Document search terms, databases, dates, and selection decisions.

Synthesis strategies

Organisation principle When useful Risk
Chronological When the field has clear historical development. May become a simple timeline.
Thematic When literature can be grouped by topic or mechanism. Requires clear categories.
Methodological When comparing analytical methods or designs. May ignore substantive findings.
Research-question based When each section supports one research question. Requires strong alignment.
Key principle The literature overview should guide the reader toward the research gap, not merely demonstrate that the author has read many sources.
Methodology

How to build the reproducibility contract

The methodology section explains how the research was conducted and why the selected approach was appropriate for answering the research questions.

Exact excerpt It is the reproducibility contract of the paper.

Core components

Component What to describe Why it matters
Research design Experimental, survey, case study, modelling, qualitative, quantitative, or mixed-methods design. Shows the logic by which evidence answers the research questions.
Study area / sample / material Location, population, cases, samples, treatments, equipment, or datasets. Defines the boundary of inference.
Data collection Measurements, observations, interviews, documents, laboratory procedures, or databases. Provides transparency and replicability.
Data analysis Statistical tests, models, coding, comparison criteria, or computational procedures. Explains how observations become results.
Validity and reliability Controls, repetitions, calibration, triangulation, or robustness checks. Strengthens trust in the results.

Methodological approaches

Approach Typical data Strength Limitation
Quantitative Measurements, numerical indicators, experimental data. Supports comparison and modelling. May reduce complex contexts to variables.
Qualitative Interviews, documents, observations, expert views. Provides depth and context. Limited generalization.
Mixed methods Combination of numerical and non-numerical evidence. Provides holistic understanding. Requires careful integration.
Avoid Do not present results in the methodology section. Methodology explains procedures and justification, not findings.
Results

Presentation of results

The results section reports findings in connection with the research questions. It should present evidence objectively, logically, and without interpretation that belongs in the discussion section.

Principles of result reporting

Principle Explanation Practical strategy
Objectivity Report findings without personal bias. Use precise, evidence-based statements.
Relevance Include only results linked to objectives and questions. Organise by objective, research question, or hypothesis.
Clarity Make the sequence easy to follow. Use subheadings, tables, and figures consistently.
Conciseness Avoid repetition and raw-data overload. Present representative data and summary statistics.

Include

  • Major findings related to research questions.
  • Positive and negative results when relevant.
  • Summary-level data and meaningful statistics.
  • Clear references to figures and tables.

Avoid

  • Interpretation of causes or mechanisms.
  • Claims that exceed the evidence.
  • Excessive raw data.
  • Repeating every number from a table.
Useful wording “Figure 2 shows...” · “Compared with the control...” · “No statistically significant difference was detected...” · “Overall, the results indicate...”
Discussion

Discussion of results and outlook

The discussion section interprets results, explains significance, compares findings with previous research, identifies limitations, and clarifies the paper’s contribution.

Results versus discussion

Results section Discussion section
Reports what was found. Explains what the findings mean.
Uses evidence, figures, tables, and statistics. Uses interpretation, comparison, and reasoning.
Avoids speculation. Offers cautious explanations supported by evidence.
Follows objectives or questions. Connects findings to literature, theory, and implications.

Recommended discussion structure

  1. Main conclusion: State the central finding in one or two sentences.
  2. Interpretation: Explain why the finding matters.
  3. Comparison: Indicate whether the result confirms, extends, or contradicts previous studies.
  4. Limitations: Explain the boundaries of the study.
  5. Implications: Discuss theoretical, methodological, or practical relevance.
  6. Outlook: Indicate future research directions or applications.

Outlook types

Outlook type Purpose Example wording
Future empirical research Indicate additional data, regions, or periods needed. “Future studies should validate these findings across additional sites.”
Methodological improvement Suggest stronger methods or measurements. “Further research could integrate additional analytical indicators.”
Practical implementation Support decision-making or application. “The results may inform sustainable management strategies.”
Key distinction Results provide evidence; discussion converts evidence into meaning, contribution, and boundary conditions.
Visual and language discipline

Tables, figures and academic language

Visual materials and academic language are not decorative. They shape how clearly readers understand the evidence, logic, and contribution of a scientific paper.

Choosing the right visual form

Visual form Best used for Common mistake
Table Exact values, classifications, comparison of variables. Using a table when a trend would be clearer in a graph.
Graph Trends, patterns, relationships, distributions. Overloading the graph with too many variables.
Flowchart Processes, workflows, methodological steps. Using inconsistent arrows or unclear sequence.
Conceptual framework Relationships among concepts or variables. Presenting an attractive but analytically vague diagram.

Academic language

Quality Meaning Example practice
Precision Use terms consistently and define key concepts. Specify the material, method, and context.
Conciseness Avoid unnecessary repetition or inflated language. Use direct, evidence-based sentences.
Caution Do not overstate conclusions. Use “suggests”, “indicates”, or “may contribute” when appropriate.
Coherence Each paragraph has one main function. Use topic sentences and logical transitions.
Practical rule Text should guide interpretation, tables should provide precision, and figures should reveal patterns.
Ethics

Citation discipline, ethics and AI use

Scientific writing depends on transparency, intellectual honesty, and responsible use of sources and tools.

Citation discipline

  • Cite sources when using ideas, methods, data, definitions, figures, or interpretations from other authors.
  • Distinguish clearly between literature-based knowledge, empirical results, and the author’s own interpretation.
  • Use a consistent reference style required by the course, journal, or institution.
  • Do not cite sources that have not been read or verified.
  • Do not use citation count alone as a proxy for quality.

Ethical risks

Risk Description Prevention
Plagiarism Using another author’s words or ideas without attribution. Paraphrase properly and cite the source.
Fabrication Inventing data, results, or references. Maintain raw records and verify all references.
Overclaiming Drawing conclusions stronger than evidence supports. Use cautious language and acknowledge limitations.
Misuse of AI Generating unverified content, fake citations, or undisclosed analysis. Use AI only as an assistant and verify outputs.
Non-negotiable rule AI must not be used to fabricate data, invent references, conceal authorship responsibilities, or replace critical scholarly judgment.
These guidelines are designed to keep evidence, interpretation, and ethics visibly aligned across the manuscript.
